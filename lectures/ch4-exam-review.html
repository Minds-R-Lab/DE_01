Topic 1: Matrix Operations Quick Review

Determinant of 2×2
$$\det\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} = ad - bc$$
Inverse of 2×2
$$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$$
Matrix Multiplication
$$[AB]_{ij} = \sum_k a_{ik}b_{kj}$$
Each element of the product is a dot product of a row and column.

⚠️ Key Warning

  • Non-commutative: $AB \neq BA$ in general!
  • Inverse doesn't exist if: $\det(A) = 0$

Topic 2: Finding Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

STEP 1 Form $A - \lambda I$ and compute its determinant
STEP 2 Solve $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$ for $\lambda$
STEP 3 For each $\lambda$, solve $(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$
Characteristic Polynomial (2×2)
$$\lambda^2 - \text{tr}(A)\lambda + \det(A) = 0$$
where $\text{tr}(A) = a + d$ (trace = sum of diagonals)
Worked Example

Find Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors: $A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$

STEP 1 Recognize: upper triangular → eigenvalues are diagonal
$$\lambda_1 = 3, \quad \lambda_2 = 2$$
STEP 2 For $\lambda_1 = 3$: solve $(A - 3I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$
$$\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \end{pmatrix} = \mathbf{0} \quad \Rightarrow \quad v_2 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$$
STEP 3 For $\lambda_2 = 2$: solve $(A - 2I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$
$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \end{pmatrix} = \mathbf{0} \quad \Rightarrow \quad v_1 + v_2 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}$$
$$\lambda_1 = 3 \text{ with } \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \lambda_2 = 2 \text{ with } \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Topic 3: Solving $\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}$ — Case 1: Distinct Real

General Solution
$$\mathbf{x}(t) = c_1e^{\lambda_1 t}\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2e^{\lambda_2 t}\mathbf{v}_2$$
where $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$ are real eigenvalues with eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2$.
Quick Example
Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = -1, \lambda_2 = 3$ with $\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}, \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}$
$$\mathbf{x}(t) = c_1e^{-t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} + c_2e^{3t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}$$
Worked Example

$\mathbf{x}' = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{x}$, $\mathbf{x}(0) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}$

STEP 1 Find eigenvalues
$$\det(A - \lambda I) = (2-\lambda)(-\lambda) - 3 = \lambda^2 - 2\lambda - 3 = (\lambda - 3)(\lambda + 1) = 0$$
$$\lambda_1 = 3, \quad \lambda_2 = -1$$
STEP 2 Find eigenvectors
$$\lambda = 3: \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 3 & -3 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \Rightarrow \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
$$\lambda = -1: \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 3 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \Rightarrow \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix}$$
STEP 3 Apply initial condition
$$c_1\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} + c_2\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} \Rightarrow c_1 + c_2 = 1, c_1 - 3c_2 = 5 \Rightarrow c_1 = 2, c_2 = -1$$
$$\mathbf{x}(t) = 2e^{3t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} - e^{-t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix}$$

Topic 4: Case 2 — Complex Eigenvalues

Complex Eigenvalues
$$\lambda = \alpha \pm \beta i$$
For eigenvector $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}i$ (with $\lambda = \alpha + \beta i$):
Real-Valued Solution
$$\mathbf{x}(t) = c_1 e^{\alpha t}[\cos(\beta t)\mathbf{a} - \sin(\beta t)\mathbf{b}] + c_2 e^{\alpha t}[\sin(\beta t)\mathbf{a} + \cos(\beta t)\mathbf{b}]$$
The solution oscillates with envelope $e^{\alpha t}$ (grows if $\alpha > 0$, decays if $\alpha < 0$).
Worked Example

$\mathbf{x}' = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & -2 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{x}$

STEP 1 Find characteristic polynomial
$$\det(A - \lambda I) = (-1-\lambda)(-1-\lambda) + 2 = \lambda^2 + 2\lambda + 3 = 0$$
STEP 2 Solve for $\lambda$
$$\lambda = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{4-12}}{2} = \frac{-2 \pm 2i\sqrt{2}}{2} = -1 \pm i\sqrt{2}$$
STEP 3 Identify $\alpha$ and $\beta$
$$\alpha = -1, \quad \beta = \sqrt{2}$$
Physical Meaning
Damped oscillation: oscillates (frequency $\sqrt{2}$) while decaying (envelope $e^{-t}$).

Topic 5: Case 3 — Repeated Eigenvalue

The Problem
When $\lambda$ is a repeated root, we might only have ONE eigenvector, but we need TWO linearly independent solutions!
Two Subcases
Subcase A (2 independent eigenvectors):
$$\mathbf{x}(t) = c_1e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v}_2$$
Subcase B (1 eigenvector): Find generalized eigenvector $\mathbf{w}$ where $(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{v}$
$$\mathbf{x}(t) = c_1e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{v} + c_2e^{\lambda t}(t\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w})$$
Worked Example

$\mathbf{x}' = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{x}$

STEP 1 Find eigenvalues
$$\det(A - \lambda I) = (3-\lambda)(1-\lambda) + 1 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 4 = (\lambda - 2)^2 = 0$$
$$\lambda = 2 \text{ (double root)}$$
STEP 2 Find eigenvector(s)
$$(A - 2I) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \Rightarrow \text{rank} = 1 \Rightarrow \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
STEP 3 Find generalized eigenvector
$$(A - 2I)\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{v} \Rightarrow \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} w_1 \\ w_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
$$w_1 - w_2 = 1 \Rightarrow \mathbf{w} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$$
$$\mathbf{x}(t) = c_1e^{2t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} + c_2e^{2t}\left(t\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\right)$$

Topic 6: Converting Higher-Order to Systems

Recipe
For $y^{(n)} + a_{n-1}y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_0 y = 0$:
Let $x_1 = y, x_2 = y', x_3 = y'', \ldots, x_n = y^{(n-1)}$
$$\mathbf{x}' = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 & \cdots \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & \cdots \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots \\ -a_0 & -a_1 & -a_2 & \cdots & -a_{n-1} \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{x}$$
Example
$y'' + 5y' + 6y = 0$ becomes:
$$\mathbf{x}' = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -6 & -5 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{x}$$

Topic 7: Phase Portrait Classification (8 Types)

Classify equilibrium $(0,0)$ by the eigenvalues of $A$:

Case 1: $\lambda_1 < \lambda_2 < 0$

Stable Node
Both negative, distinct
✓ Asymp. Stable

Case 2: $0 < \lambda_1 < \lambda_2$

Unstable Node
Both positive, distinct
✗ Unstable

Case 3: $\lambda_1 < 0 < \lambda_2$

Saddle
Opposite signs
✗ Unstable

Case 4: $\alpha \pm \beta i$, $\alpha < 0$

Stable Spiral
Complex, negative real
✓ Asymp. Stable

Case 5: $\alpha \pm \beta i$, $\alpha > 0$

Unstable Spiral
Complex, positive real
✗ Unstable

Case 6: $\pm \beta i$

Center
Pure imaginary
⊙ Stable

Case 7: $\lambda$ Double, 2 eigvecs

Star Node
All directions equally
Stability depends on sign

Case 8: $\lambda$ Double, 1 eigvec

Improper Node
Degenerate node
Stability depends on sign

Topic 8: Trace-Determinant Classification

Key Quantities
$$\tau = \text{tr}(A) = a + d, \quad \Delta = \det(A) = ad - bc$$
$$\lambda^2 - \tau\lambda + \Delta = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \lambda = \frac{\tau \pm \sqrt{\tau^2 - 4\Delta}}{2}$$
Quick Classification
  • $\Delta < 0$: Saddle (always unstable)
  • $\Delta > 0, \tau < 0$: Stable (node or spiral depending on $\tau^2 - 4\Delta$)
  • $\Delta > 0, \tau > 0$: Unstable (node or spiral)
  • $\tau = 0, \Delta > 0$: Center (oscillation, stable)
  • $\tau^2 - 4\Delta > 0$ (same sign $\tau$): Node
  • $\tau^2 - 4\Delta < 0$ (same sign $\tau$): Spiral
Worked Example

Classify these systems (no solving!):

1 $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -3 \\ 3 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$
$$\tau = 2, \quad \Delta = 1 + 9 = 10, \quad \tau^2 - 4\Delta = 4 - 40 = -36 < 0$$
$$\text{Unstable Spiral} \quad (\Delta > 0, \tau > 0, \text{ complex roots})$$
2 $A = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1 \\ 0 & -3 \end{pmatrix}$
$$\tau = -5, \quad \Delta = 6, \quad \tau^2 - 4\Delta = 25 - 24 = 1 > 0$$
$$\text{Stable Node} \quad (\Delta > 0, \tau < 0, \text{ real roots})$$
3 $A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 4 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$
$$\tau = 0, \quad \Delta = 4, \quad \text{Pure imaginary roots}$$
$$\text{Center} \quad (\tau = 0, \Delta > 0)$$

Topic 9: Two-Tank Application

Two-Tank System
$$A_1 h_1' = Q_{\text{in}} - k_1 h_1$$
$$A_2 h_2' = k_1 h_1 - k_2 h_2$$
Flow into Tank 1 minus flow out = flow into Tank 2 minus flow out.
Key Properties
  • Lower triangular matrix: eigenvalues are the diagonal entries!
  • Steady state (if $Q_{\text{in}} = 0$): $h_1^* = 0, h_2^* = 0$ (empties both tanks)
  • With inflow: $h_1^* = Q_{\text{in}}/k_1, h_2^* = Q_{\text{in}}/k_2$
  • Stability: Always asymptotically stable if $k_1, k_2 > 0$
Worked Example

Two-Tank: $A_1 = A_2 = 1$, $k_1 = k_2 = 1$, $Q_{\text{in}} = 0$, $h_1(0) = 3$, $h_2(0) = 0$

STEP 1 Set up the system
$$\mathbf{h}' = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{h}, \quad \mathbf{h}(0) = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$$
STEP 2 Find eigenvalues (lower triangular)
$$\lambda_1 = -1 \text{ (double root)}$$
STEP 3 Solve: $h_1(t) = 3e^{-t}$, $h_2(t) = 3te^{-t}$
$$h_2(t) \text{ peaks at } t = 1 \text{ with } h_2(1) = 3e^{-1} \approx 1.1$$
$$h_1(t) = 3e^{-t}, \quad h_2(t) = 3te^{-t}$$

Exam Strategy & Tips

⚠️ Best Practices

  • Check if triangular: If $A$ is upper or lower triangular, eigenvalues = diagonal entries!
  • Compute $\tau$ and $\Delta$ first: Quick classification without solving eigenvalues
  • For IVPs: Set up a linear system for constants $c_1, c_2$ — solve carefully (common error spot!)
  • Verify your solution: Plug $\mathbf{x}(t)$ back into $\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}$ to check
  • Repeated eigenvalues: Don't forget the generalized eigenvector — this is tricky!
  • Time management: Each eigenvalue problem takes 10–15 minutes. Pace yourself!
Common Mistakes to Avoid
  • Forgetting to solve $(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$ correctly
  • Mixing up $\alpha$ and $\beta$ in complex case (real vs. imaginary part)
  • Applying ICs incorrectly (matrix equation not solved right)
  • Assuming repeated eigenvalue has 2 eigenvectors without checking rank

Chapter 4 Complete Summary

Matrices & Eigenvalues

Compute $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$ to find eigenvalues. Then solve $(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$ for eigenvectors. This is the foundation of everything!

Three Cases

Case 1: Distinct real $\Rightarrow$ exponential solutions. Case 2: Complex $\Rightarrow$ oscillating solutions. Case 3: Repeated $\Rightarrow$ add $t$ factor (generalized eigenvector).

Phase Portraits

Use $\tau$ and $\Delta$ to classify without solving. Know the 8 types: stable/unstable nodes, spirals, saddles, centers, star nodes, improper nodes.

Applications

Two-tank problems, coupled springs, RLC circuits. Set up the system matrix, find eigenvalues, solve with ICs. Same method each time!
Final Thoughts
You have all the tools you need. The method is consistent: eigenvalues → eigenvectors → general solution → apply ICs. Trust the process. You've got this!
