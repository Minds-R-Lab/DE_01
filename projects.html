Group Research Projects

Discover how differential equations power your engineering specialty. Pick a topic, form a team, research it, and teach the class what you learned.

Project Objective

The goal of this project is to connect what you learn in this DE course to real-world problems in your own engineering specialty. By researching a topic where differential equations play a central role, you will see first-hand that DEs are not abstract math — they are the language that engineers use to describe, predict, and design the systems around us.

What Your Presentation Should Include

Every presentation, regardless of the specialty, should address the following:

  1. The Real-World Problem — What physical, engineering, or scientific problem are you studying? Why does it matter?
  2. The DE Model — What differential equation describes the system? Explain each term and variable.
  3. Solution or Simulation — Solve the DE analytically (if possible) or show a numerical/graphical solution. Interpret what the solution tells you.
  4. Connection to Your Major — How do engineers in your field use this model? What design decisions does it inform?
  5. What You Learned — Key takeaways and anything that surprised you.

Electrical Engineering

— 4 project ideas
EE-01

RLC Circuit Transient Response

Ch 2: 2nd-Order Moderate Analytical + Simulation
+
Background

When you connect a resistor (R), inductor (L), and capacitor (C) in series and apply a voltage, the current doesn't jump instantly to its final value — it oscillates, decays, or overshoots depending on the component values. This transient behavior is described by a second-order linear ODE with constant coefficients, the same type you study in Chapter 2.

The DE: \(L\,\frac{d^2q}{dt^2} + R\,\frac{dq}{dt} + \frac{1}{C}\,q = E(t)\), where \(q(t)\) is the charge and \(E(t)\) is the applied voltage.

What to Investigate
  • Derive the ODE from Kirchhoff's voltage law.
  • Identify the three damping regimes: overdamped, critically damped, and underdamped. What do the characteristic roots look like in each case?
  • Pick specific R, L, C values for each regime, solve the ODE, and plot the resulting current \(i(t) = dq/dt\).
  • Explain why electrical engineers care about damping — for example, in filter design or power supply smoothing.

Presentation tip: Show side-by-side plots of the three damping cases. The visual contrast is very effective for the audience.

EE-02

RC Circuit Charging and Time Constants

Ch 1: 1st-Order Introductory Analytical
+
Background

The simple RC circuit — a resistor and capacitor in series with a battery — is one of the first things EE students encounter. Charging and discharging a capacitor is governed by a first-order linear ODE, and the concept of a "time constant" \(\tau = RC\) comes directly from its solution.

The DE: \(RC\,\frac{dV_C}{dt} + V_C = V_s\), where \(V_C(t)\) is the capacitor voltage and \(V_s\) is the source voltage.

What to Investigate
  • Derive the ODE from Kirchhoff's laws. Identify it as a first-order linear ODE.
  • Solve for charging (\(V_C(0) = 0\)) and discharging (\(V_s = 0\)) cases using an integrating factor.
  • Explain the time constant \(\tau = RC\): after \(5\tau\), the capacitor is about 99% charged. Why is this important in practice?
  • Discuss applications: touch screens, camera flashes, signal filtering, debouncing circuits.

Presentation tip: Bring a real capacitor and battery (or show a short video) to demonstrate charging delay.

EE-03

Transfer Functions and Laplace Transforms in Circuit Analysis

Ch 3: Laplace Transform Advanced Analytical
+
Background

In practice, electrical engineers rarely solve circuit DEs in the time domain. Instead, they use the Laplace transform to convert the ODE into an algebraic equation in the \(s\)-domain, define a transfer function \(H(s)\), and analyze the system's frequency response. This is the foundation of signal processing and control theory.

Key idea: Take the Laplace transform of the circuit ODE to get \(H(s) = \frac{V_{out}(s)}{V_{in}(s)}\). The poles and zeros of \(H(s)\) tell you everything about the circuit's behavior.

What to Investigate
  • Choose a simple circuit (e.g., low-pass RC filter or bandpass RLC filter).
  • Write the ODE, take the Laplace transform, and derive \(H(s)\).
  • Find the poles and zeros. Explain what they mean physically (resonance, cutoff frequency, stability).
  • Plot the frequency response (magnitude vs. frequency) — also known as a Bode plot.

Presentation tip: If any team member has access to MATLAB or Python, a live Bode plot demo is very impressive.

EE-04

DC Motor Speed Control

Ch 4: Systems Advanced Modeling + Simulation
+
Background

A DC motor's speed depends on the interaction between its electrical circuit (voltage, current, inductance) and its mechanical load (inertia, friction, torque). This coupling is described by a system of two first-order ODEs — exactly the type studied in Chapter 4.

The system: \(L\,\frac{di}{dt} + Ri = V - K_b\omega\)  and  \(J\,\frac{d\omega}{dt} + b\omega = K_t i\), where \(i(t)\) is current and \(\omega(t)\) is angular velocity.

What to Investigate
  • Explain the physical meaning of each parameter: \(L\) (inductance), \(R\) (resistance), \(K_b\) (back-EMF constant), \(J\) (moment of inertia), \(b\) (friction), \(K_t\) (torque constant).
  • Write the system in matrix form and find the eigenvalues. What do they tell you about motor response?
  • Simulate the motor start-up: plot \(i(t)\) and \(\omega(t)\) from rest to steady-state.
  • Discuss how engineers use these models to design speed controllers (e.g., PID control).

Mechanical Engineering

— 4 project ideas
ME-01

Mass-Spring-Damper: Vibration Analysis

Ch 2: 2nd-Order Moderate Analytical + Simulation
+
Background

Vibration analysis is arguably the most important application of second-order ODEs in mechanical engineering. Every car suspension, every building during an earthquake, and every machine on a factory floor can be modeled as a mass-spring-damper system.

The DE: \(m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = F(t)\), where \(m\) is mass, \(c\) is damping coefficient, \(k\) is spring stiffness, and \(F(t)\) is an external force.

What to Investigate
  • Draw a free-body diagram and derive the ODE from Newton's second law.
  • Solve the free vibration case (\(F(t) = 0\)) for underdamped, critically damped, and overdamped scenarios.
  • Solve the forced vibration case with \(F(t) = F_0 \cos(\omega t)\) and explain resonance — when does the amplitude blow up?
  • Connect to real applications: car suspension tuning, earthquake-resistant building design, or washing machine balancing.
ME-02

Newton's Law of Cooling: Heat Transfer

Ch 1: 1st-Order Introductory Analytical + Experiment
+
Background

When a hot object is placed in a cooler environment, its temperature doesn't drop instantly — it decays exponentially toward the ambient temperature. Newton's law of cooling gives a first-order linear ODE that describes this process, and it's one of the simplest yet most useful models in thermal engineering.

The DE: \(\frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_{\text{env}})\), where \(T(t)\) is the object temperature, \(T_{\text{env}}\) is ambient, and \(k > 0\) is the cooling constant.

What to Investigate
  • Solve the ODE using separation of variables. Show that \(T(t) = T_{\text{env}} + (T_0 - T_{\text{env}})e^{-kt}\).
  • Run a simple experiment: measure the temperature of a cup of hot water every minute as it cools. Fit \(k\) from the data.
  • Discuss engineering applications: HVAC system design, CPU thermal management, food safety (how long until food reaches a safe temperature?).

Presentation tip: Showing your own experimental data alongside the theoretical curve is very convincing.

ME-03

Projectile Motion with Air Resistance

Ch 1: 1st-Order Moderate Analytical + Numerical
+
Background

In introductory physics, you learn projectile motion without air resistance — nice parabolas. But in reality, air drag dramatically changes the trajectory. With linear drag, the DE is first-order and solvable analytically. With quadratic drag (realistic for fast objects), you need numerical methods.

The DE (vertical, linear drag): \(m\frac{dv}{dt} = -mg - bv\), leading to terminal velocity \(v_{\text{term}} = mg/b\).

What to Investigate
  • Solve the linear drag case analytically: find velocity and position as functions of time.
  • Show that terminal velocity exists and explain its physical meaning.
  • Compare trajectories with and without air resistance. How much shorter is the range?
  • Use Euler's method (Chapter 5) to solve the quadratic drag case numerically and compare.
ME-04

Pendulum Dynamics: From Linear to Chaotic

Ch 2 + Ch 5 Advanced Analytical + Numerical
+
Background

The simple pendulum gives a nonlinear ODE: \(\ddot{\theta} + (g/L)\sin\theta = 0\). For small angles, we linearize (\(\sin\theta \approx \theta\)) and get a simple harmonic oscillator. But for large angles, the nonlinearity matters and the period changes. Adding a driving force can even produce chaos (double pendulum).

The DE: \(\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} + \frac{g}{L}\sin\theta = 0\) (nonlinear) vs. \(\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} + \frac{g}{L}\theta = 0\) (linearized).

What to Investigate
  • Solve the linearized case (Chapter 2). Find the period \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}\).
  • Use a numerical method (Euler or Runge-Kutta from Chapter 5) to solve the nonlinear case for large angles. How does the period change?
  • Compare the linear and nonlinear solutions graphically.
  • Optional: discuss the double pendulum and the concept of chaotic motion.

Civil Engineering

— 3 project ideas
CE-01

Beam Deflection Under Load

Ch 2: 2nd-Order Moderate Analytical
+
Background

When a beam carries a load, it bends. How much it bends — and where — is governed by the Euler-Bernoulli beam equation, a second-order (or fourth-order) ODE. Understanding beam deflection is fundamental to structural engineering: bridges, floors, and cranes all depend on these calculations.

The DE: \(EI\,\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = M(x)\), where \(E\) is Young's modulus, \(I\) is the moment of inertia, \(y(x)\) is the deflection, and \(M(x)\) is the bending moment.

What to Investigate
  • Explain what \(E\), \(I\), and \(M(x)\) represent physically.
  • Solve for a simply supported beam with a uniform distributed load. Find the maximum deflection.
  • Compare with a cantilever beam (different boundary conditions, different solution).
  • Discuss building codes: how much deflection is acceptable for a floor beam?
CE-02

Earthquake Response of a Single-Story Structure

Ch 2: Forced Vibrations Advanced Modeling + Simulation
+
Background

During an earthquake, the ground moves and the building responds as a forced vibration system. A single-story structure can be modeled as a single-degree-of-freedom mass-spring-damper system — the same second-order ODE from Chapter 2, but with an earthquake forcing function.

The DE: \(m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = -m\ddot{x}_g(t)\), where \(\ddot{x}_g(t)\) is the ground acceleration from the earthquake.

What to Investigate
  • Model the earthquake as a sinusoidal ground motion \(\ddot{x}_g(t) = A\sin(\omega_g t)\).
  • Solve using undetermined coefficients or Laplace transforms.
  • Investigate resonance: what happens when the earthquake frequency matches the building's natural frequency?
  • Explain how engineers design structures to avoid resonance (tuned mass dampers, base isolation).
CE-03

Mixing Tank Problem: Water Treatment

Ch 1 + Ch 4 Introductory Analytical
+
Background

In environmental and civil engineering, water treatment involves mixing tanks where pollutants enter and leave at known rates. The concentration of pollutant over time is governed by a first-order linear ODE. With multiple connected tanks, you get a system of ODEs (Chapter 4).

The DE (single tank): \(\frac{dx}{dt} = r_{in}c_{in} - \frac{r_{out}}{V}x\), where \(x(t)\) is the amount of pollutant, \(r\) are flow rates, and \(V\) is the tank volume.

What to Investigate
  • Solve the single-tank problem: how long to reduce the pollutant concentration by 90%?
  • Extend to two connected tanks (Chapter 4 system) and write the system in matrix form.
  • Discuss real-world applications: wastewater treatment plants, chemical dosing, desalination pre-treatment.

Computer Engineering / Science

— 3 project ideas
CS-01

Numerical ODE Solvers: Euler vs. Runge-Kutta

Ch 5: Numerical Methods Moderate Programming + Analysis
+
Background

Most real-world DEs can't be solved by hand. Computer scientists and engineers write algorithms that compute approximate solutions numerically. Euler's method is the simplest; the Runge-Kutta (RK4) method is far more accurate. Comparing them teaches you about accuracy, stability, and computational cost.

Key idea: Implement both methods in Python (or any language), solve a known ODE, and compare the errors at each step.

What to Investigate
  • Implement Euler's method and RK4 from scratch (no library functions).
  • Choose a test ODE with a known exact solution (e.g., \(y' = -2y\), exact: \(y = e^{-2t}\)).
  • Plot the numerical solutions against the exact solution. Measure the error.
  • Show how halving the step size affects the error for each method (error order).
  • Discuss real-world usage: what methods do modern ODE libraries (SciPy, MATLAB) actually use?
CS-02

Neural ODEs: Deep Learning Meets Differential Equations

Ch 5 + AI/ML Advanced Research + Conceptual
+
Background

In 2018, researchers showed that a deep neural network with infinitely many layers behaves like the solution of an ODE. This led to "Neural ODEs" — a cutting-edge framework where instead of stacking discrete layers, the network evolves continuously as an ODE. It's one of the hottest research areas connecting DEs to artificial intelligence.

Key idea: A residual network \(h_{t+1} = h_t + f(h_t, \theta)\) becomes, in the continuous limit, \(\frac{dh}{dt} = f(h(t), \theta)\) — an ODE solved by the network.

What to Investigate
  • Explain, at a high level, what residual networks are and how they relate to Euler's method.
  • Explain the key insight: as the number of layers approaches infinity, the network becomes an ODE.
  • Discuss advantages: memory efficiency, adaptive computation, continuous-time modeling.
  • This is a conceptual/research project — no coding required, but read and summarize the original paper (Chen et al., NeurIPS 2018).
CS-03

SIR Epidemic Model: Simulating Disease Spread

Ch 4: Systems Moderate Programming + Modeling
+
Background

The SIR model divides a population into Susceptible, Infected, and Recovered groups. The rates of change between these groups are governed by a system of nonlinear ODEs. It's the mathematical backbone behind epidemic forecasting, including the COVID-19 models that shaped public health policy worldwide.

The system: \(\frac{dS}{dt} = -\beta SI\),   \(\frac{dI}{dt} = \beta SI - \gamma I\),   \(\frac{dR}{dt} = \gamma I\).

What to Investigate
  • Explain what \(\beta\) (transmission rate) and \(\gamma\) (recovery rate) mean. What is the basic reproduction number \(R_0 = \beta/\gamma\)?
  • Write a Python script to solve the system numerically (Euler or RK4).
  • Vary \(R_0\) and show how it affects the epidemic curve. When does the epidemic die out?
  • Discuss extensions: vaccination, quarantine, age-structured models.

Industrial Engineering

— 3 project ideas
IE-01

Inventory Models with Continuous Demand

Ch 1: 1st-Order Introductory Analytical + Modeling
+
Background

In operations research, inventory levels change continuously as items are consumed and replenished. The simplest model gives a first-order ODE where the inventory level \(I(t)\) decreases at a demand rate and jumps at replenishment. Continuous approximations lead to elegant ODE models used in supply chain optimization.

The DE: \(\frac{dI}{dt} = p - d\), where \(p\) is the production rate and \(d\) is the demand rate (constant or time-varying).

What to Investigate
  • Model inventory with constant production and demand: when does inventory run out?
  • Introduce time-varying demand (e.g., seasonal) and solve the resulting ODE.
  • Connect to the Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) model and just-in-time (JIT) manufacturing.
  • Discuss how Amazon, Toyota, and other companies use these models in practice.
IE-02

Queueing Theory: Modeling Waiting Lines

Ch 1: 1st-Order Moderate Analytical + Modeling
+
Background

How long will customers wait in line at a bank? How many servers does a hospital emergency room need? Queueing theory answers these questions using DEs that describe how the number of customers in the system changes over time. The birth-death process leads to first-order ODEs for the probability of being in each state.

The DE: \(\frac{dP_n}{dt} = \lambda P_{n-1} + \mu P_{n+1} - (\lambda + \mu)P_n\), where \(\lambda\) is the arrival rate, \(\mu\) is the service rate, and \(P_n(t)\) is the probability of \(n\) customers.

What to Investigate
  • Explain the M/M/1 queue model and what each letter means.
  • Find the steady-state solution (\(dP_n/dt = 0\)) and derive the average waiting time.
  • Show what happens when the arrival rate approaches the service rate (congestion).
  • Discuss applications: hospital scheduling, call centers, airport security lines.
IE-03

Learning Curves in Manufacturing

Ch 1: Separable Introductory Analytical + Data
+
Background

Workers get faster at a task with practice — but the improvement rate decreases over time. This "learning curve" can be modeled as a separable first-order ODE. The resulting exponential approach to a maximum productivity level is used by industrial engineers to forecast labor costs and schedule production.

The DE: \(\frac{dp}{dt} = k(M - p)\), where \(p(t)\) is productivity (units/hour), \(M\) is the maximum achievable rate, and \(k > 0\) controls how fast the worker learns.

What to Investigate
  • Solve the ODE (it's separable!). Show the exponential approach to \(M\).
  • Fit the model to real or hypothetical production data.
  • Discuss how this model helps with workforce planning: how many hours of training are needed for a new hire to reach 95% efficiency?
  • Connect to Wright's learning curve law, widely used in aerospace and defense manufacturing.

Mechatronics Engineering

— 3 project ideas
MX-01

PID Controller Design Using DEs

Ch 2 + Ch 3 Advanced Modeling + Simulation
+
Background

The PID controller is the most widely used control algorithm in industry — from cruise control in cars to temperature regulation in ovens. It works by computing a control signal based on the Proportional, Integral, and Derivative of the error signal. The closed-loop system is described by a higher-order ODE, and tuning the PID gains changes its roots and hence its behavior.

Key idea: The closed-loop transfer function is a rational function in \(s\). The PID gains \((K_p, K_i, K_d)\) shift the poles, changing stability and response speed.

What to Investigate
  • Choose a simple plant model (e.g., DC motor, temperature system).
  • Write the closed-loop ODE with PID control. Find the characteristic equation.
  • Show how changing \(K_p\), \(K_i\), \(K_d\) affects the step response: overshoot, settling time, steady-state error.
  • Simulate the system (MATLAB/Python) and show the effect of tuning.
MX-02

Robot Arm Dynamics: Joint Motion

Ch 2: 2nd-Order Moderate Modeling + Analytical
+
Background

Each joint of a robot arm can be modeled as a rotational mass-spring-damper system. The motor provides a torque, joint friction acts as a damper, and the arm's inertia acts as the mass. This gives a second-order ODE for the joint angle, identical in form to the mass-spring-damper you study in Chapter 2.

The DE: \(J\ddot{\theta} + b\dot{\theta} = \tau(t)\), where \(J\) is moment of inertia, \(b\) is friction coefficient, and \(\tau(t)\) is applied torque.

What to Investigate
  • Derive the equation of motion for a single-joint robot arm.
  • Solve for the step response: apply a constant torque and find \(\theta(t)\).
  • Discuss how the solution changes with friction: too little (oscillation) vs. too much (sluggish).
  • Explain how this model is used in robot trajectory planning and joint servo design.
MX-03

Sensor Dynamics: First-Order Response of Thermocouples

Ch 1: 1st-Order Introductory Analytical + Experiment
+
Background

Sensors don't respond instantly. A thermocouple plunged into hot water takes time to reach the true temperature — this delay is described by a first-order ODE identical to Newton's law of cooling. Understanding sensor dynamics is critical in mechatronics: a slow sensor feeding a fast controller causes instability.

The DE: \(\tau\frac{dT_s}{dt} + T_s = T_{\text{actual}}\), where \(T_s\) is the sensor reading, \(T_{\text{actual}}\) is the true temperature, and \(\tau\) is the sensor's time constant.

What to Investigate
  • Solve the first-order linear ODE for a step input (sudden temperature change).
  • Explain the time constant \(\tau\): after \(3\tau\), the sensor reads 95% of the true value.
  • Discuss why fast sensors matter in control systems (bandwidth vs. noise trade-off).
  • Optional: run an experiment with a thermometer in hot water and fit \(\tau\).

Mathematics

MA-01

Existence and Uniqueness: When Solutions Break Down

Ch 1: Theory Advanced Theoretical
+
Background

Does every ODE have a solution? Is it unique? The Picard-Lindelöf theorem gives conditions under which a unique solution exists. But what happens when these conditions fail? You get equations with no solution, multiple solutions, or solutions that blow up in finite time. This is beautiful, deep mathematics.

Key theorem: If \(f(t, y)\) and \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\) are continuous near \((t_0, y_0)\), then \(y' = f(t, y)\), \(y(t_0) = y_0\) has a unique local solution.

What to Investigate
  • State and explain the Picard-Lindelöf (existence-uniqueness) theorem.
  • Give an example where uniqueness fails: \(y' = \sqrt{y}\), \(y(0) = 0\) has infinitely many solutions.
  • Give an example of finite-time blow-up: \(y' = y^2\), \(y(0) = 1\) has solution \(y = 1/(1-t)\) which explodes at \(t = 1\).
  • Discuss why engineers care: if a model has non-unique solutions, which one does nature "choose"?
MA-02

Power Series Solutions and Special Functions

Ch 5: Series Moderate Analytical
+
Background

Many important ODEs (Bessel's equation, Legendre's equation, Airy's equation) cannot be solved in terms of elementary functions. Instead, mathematicians define new "special functions" as power series solutions. These functions appear everywhere in physics and engineering — from the vibrations of a drum to the quantum mechanics of the hydrogen atom.

Example — Bessel's equation: \(x^2y'' + xy' + (x^2 - n^2)y = 0\). Its solutions \(J_n(x)\) are the Bessel functions, used in acoustics, electromagnetics, and heat conduction.

What to Investigate
  • Choose one special function (Bessel, Legendre, Hermite, or Airy).
  • Write the ODE, find the power series solution (use the Frobenius method from Chapter 5).
  • Plot the function and describe its properties (zeros, oscillation, orthogonality).
  • Give at least two real-world applications of this special function.
MA-03

Phase Portraits and Stability of Nonlinear Systems

Ch 4: Phase Portraits Advanced Analytical + Graphical
+
Background

For a system of two ODEs, the phase portrait shows all possible trajectories in the \((x, y)\) plane. The classification of equilibrium points (nodes, spirals, saddles, centers) reveals the qualitative behavior without solving anything. This is the gateway to nonlinear dynamics and chaos theory.

Key idea: Classify the equilibrium \((0,0)\) of \(\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x}\) by the eigenvalues of \(A\). Real, distinct → node; complex → spiral; pure imaginary → center; opposite sign → saddle.

What to Investigate
  • Draw phase portraits for each type of equilibrium (use software or draw by hand).
  • Explain what the eigenvalues tell you about stability.
  • Apply linearization to a nonlinear system (e.g., predator-prey or Van der Pol oscillator).
  • Discuss why phase portraits are useful: engineers can predict long-term behavior without solving the ODE.

Mathematics Education

— 3 project ideas
ED-01

Population Growth Models: From Malthus to Logistic

Ch 1: Separable Introductory Modeling + Historical
+
Background

Population growth is one of the most intuitive introductions to differential equations. Malthus proposed exponential growth (\(P' = rP\)), but Verhulst improved it with the logistic model (\(P' = rP(1 - P/K)\)) that accounts for resource limits. These models are perfect for teaching because students can relate to them and compare with real data.

The DEs: Exponential: \(\frac{dP}{dt} = rP\)  →  Logistic: \(\frac{dP}{dt} = rP\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right)\). Both are separable!

What to Investigate
  • Solve both DEs analytically. Show that the logistic model has an S-shaped curve.
  • Fit the models to real population data (world population, bacteria growth, etc.).
  • Discuss when exponential is a good approximation and when it fails.
  • Design a lesson plan: how would you teach this to high school or first-year university students?

Presentation tip: Compare your logistic prediction with actual world population data — it's surprisingly accurate up to about 2050!

ED-02

Radioactive Decay and Carbon-14 Dating

Ch 1: Separable Introductory Analytical + Application
+
Background

Radioactive decay is governed by the simplest separable ODE: the rate of decay is proportional to the amount present. The half-life concept and carbon-14 dating are direct consequences. This topic connects DEs to archaeology, geology, medicine (radioactive tracers), and nuclear engineering — making it ideal for education students.

The DE: \(\frac{dN}{dt} = -\lambda N\), with solution \(N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}\) and half-life \(t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda}\).

What to Investigate
  • Solve the ODE and derive the half-life formula.
  • Work a carbon-14 dating example: estimate the age of an artifact given its remaining C-14 fraction.
  • Discuss other isotopes and their applications (medical imaging with technetium-99m, uranium dating for rocks).
  • Design a classroom activity: how would you make this topic engaging for students?
ED-03

Slope Fields as a Visual Teaching Tool

Ch 1 + Ch 5 Introductory Visual + Pedagogical
+
Background

Many students struggle with DEs because they're abstract. Slope fields provide a visual, geometric way to understand solutions without solving anything. At each point \((t, y)\), you draw a small line segment with slope \(f(t, y)\). Solution curves "flow" along these slopes. This tool is powerful for building intuition.

Key idea: For \(y' = f(t, y)\), draw the slope \(f(t, y)\) at a grid of points. Solutions are curves tangent to these slopes everywhere.

What to Investigate
  • Draw slope fields (by hand or with software) for several DEs: \(y' = y\), \(y' = t - y\), \(y' = y(1 - y)\).
  • Overlay actual solution curves on the slope fields. Show how different initial conditions lead to different solutions.
  • Explain how slope fields reveal qualitative behavior: equilibrium solutions, stability, asymptotic behavior.
  • Design an interactive classroom activity where students draw slope fields by hand and predict solutions.
